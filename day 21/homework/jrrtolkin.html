J.R.R. Tolkien (John Ronald Reuel Tolkien) was an English writer, professor, philologist, and academic, best known for his high-fantasy works The Hobbit and The Lord of the Rings trilogy. His work has had a profound influence on the genre of fantasy literature, shaping its modern form and creating some of the most iconic works in literary history. Early Life: Tolkien was born on January 3, 1892, in Bloemfontein, South Africa, where his father, Arthur, worked as a bank manager. When Tolkien was just 3 years old, his father died, and he moved with his mother and brother back to England. They settled in the West Midlands, where Tolkien developed a lifelong love for languages, mythology, and storytelling. His mother, who was a devout Roman Catholic, also had a significant influence on his religious and moral outlook. Tolkien attended Exeter College, Oxford, where he studied Classics and English literature. It was during his time at Oxford that he became interested in languages, particularly Old English, which would later influence his creative writing. He became a professor of Anglo-Saxon at Oxford, where he taught from 1925 to 1959. Linguistic Genius and Mythology: Tolkien was a skilled philologist (someone who studies languages and their history) and invented several languages over his lifetime, most famously Quenya and Sindarin, the two main languages spoken by the Elves in his fictional Middle-earth. His love of language and myth was integral to his writing, and he created entire cultures, histories, and mythologies to give depth and realism to his fictional world. His interest in mythology and folklore was also a driving force in his work. He was influenced by Nordic sagas, medieval literature, and Christian theology, which all shaped the rich, multi-layered narratives he created.
